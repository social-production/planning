# Production

## Overview

Social Production exists to organise the collective production and distribution of goods and services outside of capitalist market structures. Production on the platform is for use — not for exchange, not for profit, not for sale. What is produced belongs to the community and is distributed according to need and agreed plans.

Production does not require participants to abandon their existing lives. In its early stages the platform operates asymmetrically alongside capitalist production — people can maintain their jobs and livelihoods while gradually incorporating more productive activity into the platform. The goal is to expand the sphere of communal production over time until needs can be met outside the market entirely.

---

## Priority Production Spheres

The platform is designed to support production across all domains, but certain areas have been identified as foundational — the needs that must be met for people to feel safe transitioning away from dependence on capitalist systems. These are the areas where organised collective production has the greatest immediate impact:

**Food** — growing, processing, and distributing food to meet the nutritional needs of the community. Understanding how much needs to be produced and building the supply and distribution infrastructure to meet it.

**Housing** — collectively acquiring, building, and maintaining housing outside of property market structures. Providing stable, secure housing as a community right rather than a commodity.

**Healthcare** — organising access to care, pooling resources for medical needs, and building community health infrastructure. Addressing both immediate needs and longer-term conditions requiring sustained support.

**Communications** — maintaining the platform itself and the infrastructure supporting it. Ensuring the network remains operational and accessible to those inside and outside the system.

**Innovation** — supporting the development of new tools, methods, and projects that expand the community's productive capacity. Encouraging individual and collective experimentation without market incentives as the driver.

These spheres are not exclusive — the platform supports any form of collective production the community organises. But building strength in these areas first is what creates the conditions for broader participation.

## Project Lifecycle

Every production effort on the platform follows a defined lifecycle. Each stage is recorded on-chain, providing a transparent and immutable record of the project's history.

**Researching** — the project is proposed and the community is gauging interest. Members signal their support and desired involvement. Demand for the output starts to become visible. The community checks for existing similar projects before committing to a new one.

**Funding** — if materials, equipment, or resources need to be acquired, a fund drive is opened. Contributions are held in escrow by the foundation until the goal is met. No individual holds funds at any point. If the funding goal is not met, contributions are returned.

**Producing** — active production is underway. Tasks are tracked collectively — what needs to be done, what is in progress, what is complete. Participation is visible at an aggregate level for planning purposes without attributing individual labor value.

**Completed** — production is finished and output is ready for distribution.

**Distributing** — the agreed distribution plan is executed. Outputs are allocated to recipients according to the plan voted on by the project membership. Physical goods are shipped, digital goods are delivered by download or email, library items are made available for borrowing.

**Distributed** — the distribution cycle is complete. The project record remains transparent and available as a reference for future similar projects.

---

## Task Tracking

Active projects use a structured task tracking system — similar in function to a project board — where work is broken down into defined tasks, assigned status (to do, in progress, done), and tracked collectively.

This serves two purposes. First, it gives the project clear operational visibility — members can see what is happening, what needs doing, and where help is needed. Second, it produces aggregate data on production activity — how many people were involved, how long things took, what was achieved — that feeds into planning future projects.

Individual identity is not attached to labor metrics in any way that affects distribution or governance. Tracking what gets done is for collective planning, not for calculating individual entitlement. There is no labor credit, no token, no stored contribution that can be exchanged later.

---

## Demand Signals and Planning

Production on the platform is informed by visible community need rather than by price signals. Several mechanisms feed into what gets produced:

**Project suggestion list** — a community-maintained list of projects that members want to see happen. Any member can add to it, vote on suggestions, and pick up a suggestion to turn into a project. Projects can be suggested spontaneously, the suggestion list is just that, suggestions.

**Waitlists** — for goods where demand exceeds current production capacity, waitlists are publicly visible. A long waitlist is a direct signal that more production of that type is needed. Housing waitlists, food distribution waitlists, and similar queues are not hidden administrative records — they are planning data visible to the whole community.

**Failed project signals** — when a project fails or is cancelled, the unmet need it was addressing feeds back into the suggestion list automatically, ensuring the demand is not lost.

**Distribution history** — past production and distribution data informs estimates for future projects — how much was needed, how long it took to produce, where shortfalls occurred.

These signals together build a picture of community need that informs what gets proposed, funded, and built next — replacing the role that price plays in market economies with transparent democratic planning.

---

## Collective Ownership of Outputs

Everything produced through the platform belongs to the community. Materials acquired through collective funding, outputs produced through collective labor, and infrastructure built through collective effort are all held in trust by the foundation on the community's behalf.

No individual or group acquires private ownership over collectively produced outputs. The foundation holds legal title but has no beneficial interest — it is a steward, not an owner. The community governs what happens to what it produces through the same governance processes that govern everything else on the platform.

---

## Transition Model

The platform does not require a revolutionary break from existing systems to begin. It is designed to grow alongside capitalist production, drawing productive activity out of the market gradually as the community's capacity expands.

In the early stages, participants may be employed outside the platform and using the platform for supplementary production — growing food collectively, sharing tools, running community services. Over time, as more productive spheres are brought into the platform and basic needs become increasingly met outside the market, dependence on capitalist employment can reduce.

The goal is to build enough productive capacity and community resilience that participation in the capitalist economy becomes a choice rather than a necessity. Each project that successfully produces and distributes something outside the market is a demonstration that this is possible and a foundation for the next step.

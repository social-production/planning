# Distribution

## Overview

All produce from projects on the Social Production platform is distributed directly to community members based on need and agreed plans — not exchanged, not sold, not priced. Nothing passes through the market. Production is organised for use.

Labor contribution does not factor into distribution. Weighting distribution by individual labor input would recreate labor-power as a value form — essentially recreating capitalism. The principle is: from each according to ability, to each according to need. Distribution is governed solely by need and community-agreed plans.

Production activity is tracked collectively — numbers of people involved, time taken, quantities produced — as data to inform future planning. Individual identity is not attached to these figures and they carry no bearing on what anyone receives.

---

## Distribution Types

When a project is created, members signal how the output should be handled. Two models are available:

**Library** — borrow and return. For durable goods like tools, equipment, and shared spaces. The item stays collectively held. Members request access and return it after use. Suited to anything that can be reused without being consumed.

**Distribution** — receive a share. For consumables like food, materials, and supplies. Output is divided and allocated to recipients according to the agreed plan.

A project may use both models for different outputs. Signals from members during the planning phase shape which model the distribution plan is built around. If a tool receives 10 library signals and 2 distribution signals, the plan reflects that it stays collectively held with borrowing access rather than being divided up.

---

## Plan Submission and Voting

1. A project is created and members express interest through signals
2. A detailed plan is submitted covering location, required materials, and expected produce
3. Members submit proposals for distribution — specifying type (library or distribution), quantities, recipient criteria, and scaling procedures
4. Members vote on all submitted proposals using the platform-wide ratio voting system
5. The winning plan is adopted and recorded on-chain

All rules established through this process can be altered by the same process. The platform is designed to learn and improve through trial and error.

---

## Scaling Procedures

Every distribution plan for finite or variable output must include a scaling clause, agreed in advance by vote, so there is no dispute when shortfalls or surpluses occur.

For shortfalls, options include:

- Proportional reduction across all recipients
- Priority tiers protected first — for example, a community kitchen is made whole before remaining output is scaled
- A buffer built from surplus harvests to smooth out future shortfalls

For surpluses, the plan should specify whether excess goes into a community buffer, is redistributed proportionally, or triggers a new distribution round.

These rules are set before production begins, recorded on-chain, and apply automatically. Agreed in advance, no ambiguity.

## Indivisible Goods — Housing Model

Goods that cannot be scaled proportionally require a different approach. Housing is the primary example — you cannot give someone half a house.

**Need-based priority first.** People in genuine housing crisis go to the front regardless of anything else. The community defines and votes on what constitutes priority need — homelessness, severe overcrowding, domestic safety situations, disability requirements. Priority criteria are transparent and on-chain.

**Need verification.** Priority claims use a tiered privacy model. The claim itself is public. Supporting details are encrypted and accessible only to a small review panel, accountable to community signals and eligible for removal at any time by vote. The panel verifies the claim and records their decision on-chain without exposing private details publicly. False priority claims are a governance violation with consequences defined and voted on by the community. Channel moderators may also assist in verification where appropriate.

**Waitlist after priority need is met.** Remaining units go to a transparent first-come-first-served waitlist. Everyone can see their position and the criteria. No hidden allocations.

**Waitlist as planning signal.** Waitlist data is visible platform-wide. A long housing waitlist is a direct signal that the community should be proposing more housing projects. Demand data informs future production planning automatically — the platform connects unmet need to new project proposals organically.

**Periodic review.** Housing situations change. Allocations are reviewed on a set cycle through the governance process. Members whose need has reduced may be asked to move so someone in greater need can take the space. This is done through governance, not unilaterally.

## Delivery — Physical and Digital Goods

Distribution handles both physical and digital produce. The delivery mechanism is determined by the nature of the product and recorded against each distribution allocation.

**Physical goods** — delivered to a member's registered address. The distribution tracks a full status lifecycle: preparing → shipped → received. A member confirms receipt on the platform, closing the distribution record. Items can be returned, which is also tracked.

**Digital goods** — delivered via download link or email. The status lifecycle is shorter: preparing → downloaded. Once a member downloads the file the distribution is complete.

**Library items** — borrowable goods follow the same physical delivery flow where applicable, with a return path back to collective stock.

All three delivery types — physical address, email, and download link — are supported against a single distribution record, meaning a project can produce both physical and digital outputs and distribute them through the same system. Status changes are appended as new records rather than overwritten, giving a full immutable history of every distribution event consistent with the platform's transparency principles.

---

## Failed or Dissolved Projects

If a project fails or dissolves before completion, committed funds held in escrow by the foundation are returned to contributors. No individual ever holds community funds at any point — all money sits with the foundation until a goal is met or returned if it is not.

The on-chain record documents the failure and its circumstances transparently. Members are not penalised but the project history remains visible. The community can propose a successor project if the need remains. Unmet demand from a failed project feeds back into the platform's project suggestion list as a signal for future organising.

---

## Production Tracking

All production activity is tracked in the platform database, with key authorization and distribution events recorded on-chain. This includes:

- What was produced and in what quantity
- Collective participation data — numbers involved, time taken — for future planning purposes
- Who received what and when
- Confirmation signals from recipients that they received their allocation
- Any deviations from the agreed plan and the governance decisions that authorized them

This provides full transparency on the collective production process without attaching individual labor contribution to distribution outcomes.

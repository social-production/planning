# Social Production Operations Model

This document is the current working draft for how Social Production governs projects, organizes production, and distributes outputs.

It combines the older split between governance, distribution, and production drafts so the operating model can be reviewed in one place.

## Overview

Social Production exists to organize collective production and distribution outside capitalist market structures. Production on the platform is for use, not for exchange, not for profit, and not for sale. What is produced belongs to the community and is distributed according to need and agreed plans.

The platform itself is the organization. There are no separate membership tiers, no permanent authorities, and no governance bodies outside transparent on-chain processes. Funding a project gives no additional governance authority. Contributions go to the collective, not to influence.

## Core Principles

- Collective ownership of outputs and infrastructure
- Need-based distribution rather than exchange
- Transparent governance through on-chain records
- Revocable roles rather than permanent authority
- Production planning based on visible demand signals
- Trial, iteration, and revision through governance rather than fixed doctrine

## Projects

A project can be created by any user on the platform. The creator becomes its first manager. Governance then happens through project membership.

Each project has an attached forum or chat so members can communicate continuously throughout the full project lifecycle. This is where people discuss ideas, refine plans, coordinate work, respond to problems, and review outcomes as the project develops.

### Project Types

- Production: collective manufacture, growing, building
- Service: skills, labor, care, maintenance, mutual aid, and direct need support

Mutual aid requests such as emergency support or help moving fit within the service category and are treated as valid productive activity.

### Roles

Manager responsibilities:

- Edit the project
- Post updates
- Manage events
- Make proposals
- Vote on proposals
- Create or edit collective fund drives
- Propose role changes

Member responsibilities:

- Make proposals
- Vote on proposals

Any user can join a project and become a member. Role changes, including promoting a member to manager or removing a manager, require a passed vote. No project role is permanent.

## Voting Model

Every platform decision uses the same broad voting mechanism, whether the decision concerns project governance, distribution plans, moderation, role changes, or wider platform matters.

### Ratio-Based Voting

Members can vote yes, no, or abstain on all competing proposals independently. The outcome is determined by the broadest genuine support rather than a simple highest raw total.

A proposal must:

1. Meet the required participation threshold.
2. Be ranked by approval ratio, calculated as yes votes divided by total votes cast on that proposal.

If multiple proposals clear the threshold, the highest approval ratio wins.

### Participation Thresholds

Current planning thresholds for project decisions are:

- Fewer than 10 members: 100% participation
- 10 to 50 members: 60% participation
- 51 to 100 members: 40% participation
- 101 to 999 members: 25% participation
- 1000 or more members: 10% participation

High-stakes decisions may also require an absolute minimum vote count in addition to the percentage threshold. The exact absolute minimums still need final confirmation.

### Deadlock and Failure

If no proposal clears the threshold, the voting period extends automatically. If the threshold is still not met, the matter returns to discussion before resubmission.

## Proposal Types

The following decisions require a passed proposal vote:

- Changes to project rules or structure
- Distribution plan selection
- Promoting a member to manager
- Removing a manager
- Removing a member from a project
- Creating or modifying a fund drive
- Moderation actions above routine housekeeping
- Director appointments and removals at the foundation level

## Moderation and Organizational Scope

Posts and projects are tagged to channels representing productive spheres, topics, or locations. This tagging system helps discovery, self-organization, and moderation.

Moderation is designed as a delegated function, not an authority class. Moderator roles are revocable and accountable to the community.

### Moderation Principles

- Full exclusion is a last resort because platform access is tied to participation in production
- Harm reduction and accountability are preferred over social ostracism
- Moderation actions are reviewable and recorded on-chain where appropriate
- Voting privacy is preserved for ordinary users, while moderator standing and support should remain visible enough for accountability

### Escalation Path

Preferred responses to harmful behavior, in order of severity:

1. Community reporting and review
2. Temporary posting restrictions for a defined period
3. Removal from specific projects by member vote
4. Platform-wide restrictions through governance
5. Platform-wide ban for the most serious and sustained harmful behavior

The exact scope of moderators, for example channel-based versus category-based moderation, still needs final review. The current system design should stay flexible enough to support either model until that decision is locked.

## Production Priorities

The platform supports production across all domains, but some spheres are strategically foundational because they reduce dependence on capitalist systems most directly.

Priority spheres include:

- Food
- Housing
- Healthcare
- Communications
- Innovation

These areas are not exclusive. They are the most important early focus because they create the material conditions for broader participation.

## Project Lifecycle

Every project follows a visible lifecycle.

### Researching

The project is proposed and the community gauges interest. This stage is where the most important early demand signal appears on the project itself. Users can signal interest with a simple action on the project, indicating support, need, or likely involvement. Similar existing projects should be surfaced to encourage collaboration instead of duplicate efforts.

### Planning

Productive plans and distributive plans are submitted, discussed, revised, and voted on. Only after agreed plans are finalized does the project move to the next stage.

### Funding

If materials or equipment are required, a fund drive is opened. Contributions are held in escrow by the foundation until the goal is met. If the goal fails, contributions are returned.

### Producing

Active production is underway. Tasks are tracked collectively so members can see what is happening, what is blocked, and where help is needed.

### Completed

Production is finished and output is ready for allocation.

### Distributing

The approved distribution plan is executed and tracked.

### Distributed

The distribution cycle is complete. The full record remains available as planning input for future projects.

## Task Tracking

Active projects use structured task tracking similar to a project board, where work is broken into tasks and statuses such as to do, in progress, and done.

This serves two purposes:

- Operational visibility for the project itself
- Aggregate planning data for future projects

Individual labor is not converted into entitlement. Labor tracking exists for planning, not for exchange or credit.

## Distribution Model

All outputs are distributed directly to community members based on need and agreed plans. Labor contribution does not determine distribution.

### Distribution Types

Two models are available when planning output distribution:

- Library: borrow and return for durable goods such as tools, equipment, and shared spaces
- Distribution: receive a share for consumables such as food, materials, and supplies

A project may use both models for different outputs.

### Plan Submission and Adoption

1. A project is created and members express interest through signals.
2. A detailed plan is submitted covering location, required materials, and expected produce.
3. Members submit proposals covering quantities, recipient criteria, distribution type, and scaling procedures.
4. Members vote using the platform-wide ratio voting system.
5. The winning plan is adopted and recorded.

### Scaling Rules

Every finite or variable output plan should define what happens in shortfall and surplus conditions before production begins.

Shortfall options can include:

- Proportional reduction across recipients
- Using a pre-agreed emergency response procedure for urgent cases
- Using buffer stock built from prior surpluses

Surplus options can include:

- Community buffers
- Proportional redistribution
- A new distribution round

These rules should be agreed in advance and recorded so there is no ambiguity during execution.

### Indivisible Goods

Some goods cannot be reduced proportionally. Housing is the clearest example.

The current model is:

- Access handled through a visible waitlist
- Waitlist length used as a planning signal for future projects
- Project plans define in advance how genuine emergency situations are handled without creating a standing priority-needs distribution system for ordinary allocation
- Allocations reviewed periodically through governance rather than unilateral decision-making

This is not a permanent priority-needs model for distribution. Emergency handling should be built into the project plan itself as a clear, limited procedure for exceptional situations.

### Delivery

Distribution must support:

- Physical goods delivered to a registered address
- Digital goods delivered through download or email
- Library items with return flows back into collective stock

Distribution records should preserve a status history instead of overwriting prior states.

## Demand Signals and Planning

Production is informed by visible need rather than price signals. The most immediate signal is direct interest shown on a project while it is still in the researching stage.

Key planning inputs include:

- Project interest signals during the researching stage
- Project suggestion lists
- Waitlists
- Failed project signals
- Distribution history
- Production history and aggregate effort data

These signals help the community identify what should be proposed, funded, and built next.

## Failed or Dissolved Projects

If a project fails before completion, funds held in escrow by the foundation are returned to contributors. The failure remains visible in the record and unmet need feeds back into future planning.

## Collective Ownership

Everything produced through the platform belongs to the community. Materials acquired through collective funding, outputs produced through collective labor, and infrastructure built through collective effort are held in trust by the foundation on the community's behalf.

No individual acquires private ownership over collectively produced outputs by default.

## Transition Model

The platform does not require an immediate break from existing systems. It is designed to grow alongside capitalism at first, gradually reducing dependence on it as productive capacity grows.

Early participation may look like supplementary production such as food distribution, tool libraries, housing projects, or community services. Over time, more needs can be met outside the market, making capitalist participation less necessary for survival.

The goal is to make participation in capitalism a choice rather than a necessity.

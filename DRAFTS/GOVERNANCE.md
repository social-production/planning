# Governance

## Overview

The platform is governed entirely by its community. There are no permanent authorities, no bodies with independent power, and no decisions made outside of transparent on-chain processes. All governance actions are recorded on the blockchain.

The platform itself is the organisation. There are no separate membership tiers or organisational structures above the community. Any user can participate. Funding a project gives no additional governance authority — contributions go to the collective, not to the contributor's influence.

---

## Projects

A project is created by any user on the platform. The creator becomes its first manager. All subsequent governance happens through the membership.

**Roles**

- **Manager** — can edit the project, post updates, manage events, make proposals, vote on proposals, create or edit collective fund drives, and propose role changes
- **Member** — can make proposals and vote on proposals

Any user can join a project and become a member. Role changes — promoting a member to manager or removing a manager including the original creator — require a passed vote. No role is permanent or beyond accountability.

---

## Project Types

Projects are categorised to aid discovery and governance:

- **Production** — collective manufacture, growing, building
- **Service** — skills, labor, care, maintenance, mutual aid, direct need support


Mutual aid requests — help moving, emergency support, immediate need — are valid project types within the service category and are treated as any other productive activity.

## Voting — Platform-Wide Ratio System

Every decision on the platform uses the same voting mechanism, whether it is a distribution plan, a governance proposal, a role change, a moderation action, or any other matter requiring community decision.

**How it works**

Members can vote on all competing proposals simultaneously — yes, no, or abstain on each one independently. A member who votes yes on multiple proposals contributes meaningfully to each. The outcome is not determined by who gets the most votes but by who gets the broadest genuine support.

A proposal must meet a minimum participation threshold to be eligible. Below that threshold it is not considered regardless of its ratio. Above the threshold, proposals are ranked by approval ratio — yes votes divided by total votes cast on that proposal. The highest ratio wins.

**Example with numbers**

A project has 100 members. Three distribution plans are submitted. The participation threshold is 20% — a minimum of 20 votes required. Every member can vote on all three.

- Plan A: 60 votes — 54 yes, 6 no — **90% approval**
- Plan B: 80 votes — 64 yes, 16 no — **80% approval**
- Plan C: 25 votes — 22 yes, 3 no — **88% approval**

All three clear the threshold. Plan A wins at 90% despite fewer total votes than Plan B. A member who voted yes on all three has had their preference registered on each. A member who voted yes on B and no on A has had their preference registered too.

**Why this matters**

This finds the outcome most people are genuinely happy with, not just the outcome the loudest group wanted. It rewards proposals written for broad appeal over factional ones, reduces resentment from losing sides, and ensures people can commit to executing a decision they broadly supported rather than one they simply lost.

**Threshold scaling**

Thresholds scale to the significance of the decision and the size of the project. A project-wide governance vote may require 30% participation. A routine operational decision may require only 10%. Thresholds for each decision type are set by community vote in advance.

For small projects, minimum absolute voter counts apply to high-stakes decisions — a project of 5 members should not remove a manager on a single vote.

**Deadlock**

If no proposal clears the threshold, the voting period extends automatically. If it still fails, the decision returns to community discussion before resubmission.

## Proposal Types

The following decisions require a passed proposal vote:

- Changes to project rules or structure
- Distribution plan selection
- Promoting a member to manager
- Removing a manager
- Removing a member from the project
- Creating or modifying a fund drive
- Moderation actions at project level
- Director appointments and removals at foundation level

## Channels

Posts and projects are tagged to channels representing productive spheres, topics, or locations. Channels provide the primary organisational structure for the feed and for moderation.

A post can belong to multiple channels. The tagging system means content self-sorts by relevance — projects naturally find the communities most affected by them.

**Channel moderators** are elected by the community of each channel and are accountable to community signals within that channel. They are eligible for removal at any time, requiring a sustained ratio of positive signals to remain in their role. No moderator position is permanent. Moderators are not an authority class — they are a delegated function, revocable at will.

---

## Moderation

**Core principle:** this platform is infrastructure for socialized production. A full ban separates someone from the means of production, which is a serious harm and should not be taken lightly. Exclusion is a last resort, not a default response.

Preferred responses to harmful behaviour, in order of escalating severity:

1. Community reporting to channel moderators for review
2. Temporary posting restrictions for a defined period
3. Removal from specific projects by member vote
4. Platform-wide restrictions through community governance process
5. Platform-wide ban for the most serious and sustained harmful behaviour

Voting and participation rights are preserved wherever possible even under other restrictions. The goal is accountability and protection of the community, not social ostracisation.

All moderation actions are recorded on-chain. Moderation rules are not fixed — they can be altered through governance as the platform learns what works.

**Identity and bans**

Platform-level bans are enforced by incorporating device ID into user identity. A banned user would need a different device to circumvent a ban. This is a last resort measure only.

## Duplicate Projects

When a new project is submitted that substantially overlaps with an existing one, the platform surfaces the existing project and suggests collaboration rather than duplication. An automated similarity check assists with this for both location-based and non-location-based projects. Members are encouraged to check what is already happening before submitting — the platform supports this with clear discovery tools. Consolidating efforts into one project is preferred over fragmentation wherever the community agrees it makes sense.

---

## Project Suggestion List

The community maintains a shared list of projects that members want to see happen but that have not yet been created. This acts as a demand signal — unmet needs surface as suggestions that any member can pick up and turn into a project. Failed projects and long waitlists feed into this list automatically.
